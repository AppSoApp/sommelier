# Notes on Everyday Software Craft

## Overview

Most of the day-to-day feel of working in a codebase comes not from any single
clever design decision but from a thousand small, consistent choices: what a
variable is called, how a folder is organized, how a comment explains itself, how a
pull request describes what it changes. None of these choices is dramatic on its
own. Together they decide whether a new teammate can find their way around in an
afternoon or a week, and whether a change six months from now is a small, calm edit
or an archaeological dig. This note collects a few habits worth keeping in mind:
naming, organization, comments and documentation, and the everyday etiquette of
reading one another's changes.

## Naming

A name is the cheapest form of documentation there is, and also the one everybody
reads whether they mean to or not. A few habits go a long way. Prefer a name that
says what a thing is for over one that says how it happens to be implemented right
now — `retryDelayMs` ages better than `sleepFor250`. Keep units in the name when a
bare number could be misread: milliseconds, bytes, degrees. Boolean names read best
as a question with an obvious yes/no shape — `isEnabled`, `hasPermission`,
`shouldRetry` — rather than a bare adjective that leaves the polarity to guesswork.
Collections read more naturally in the plural, and a single item pulled from one
reads more naturally in the singular, so a loop over `orders` naming its variable
`order` tends to read better than a loop that keeps calling everything `orders` or
`item`.

Abbreviations are a tax on everyone who joins later. `usr`, `cfg`, and `tmp` save a
few keystrokes for the person typing them and cost a small guessing exercise for
every future reader; spelling the word out is almost always worth the extra
characters, with a short allow-list of abbreviations so universal they're not
really abbreviations anymore (`id`, `url`, `html`). Two names that differ only in a
suffix like `Manager` versus `Handler` versus `Service` should mean something
different, or one of them should go — a project with three near-synonymous suffixes
sprinkled at random makes every new name a small research question instead of an
obvious choice. When a concept already has a name elsewhere in the codebase, reuse
it; a second name for the same idea is a standing invitation for the two to drift
apart in a reader's head even when the code underneath stays the same.

## Organizing files and modules

A module earns its name when everything inside it is there for one reason. A file
that grows because it's convenient — "there's already an import for this" — rather
than because it's cohesive tends to become the file nobody wants to touch, because
touching it means understanding everything else that ended up there along the way.
Splitting by responsibility rather than by type (all the "utils" in one place, all
the "helpers" in another) usually keeps things easier to find later, because the
question a reader is asking is almost always "where does the code that does X
live," not "where do we keep small functions."

Import ordering, folder depth, and where configuration lives are all matters of
convention rather than any deeper design principle, which is exactly why it's worth
settling on one convention and sticking to it — the value isn't in any one choice being better than
another, it's in not having to relearn the layout for every new corner of the
project. A short `CONTRIBUTING` note describing the layout, updated when the layout
changes, saves the conversation from repeating in every review.

## Comments and documentation

The best comment explains a decision a reader couldn't get just by reading the
code: why this approach and not the obvious alternative, what constraint forced an
otherwise odd-looking shape, what would need to change if an assumption stopped
holding. A comment that just restates the line below it in English adds reading
time without adding information, and it rots the moment the code changes and the
comment doesn't. A docstring earns its keep by describing what a function is for
and what its caller can rely on — its inputs, its output, and its side effects, if
any — in language a caller can use without opening the function body.

Documentation ages fastest at the seams: the README's "getting started" section,
the comment that used to describe a parameter that has since been renamed, the
architecture diagram drawn before the last redesign. Treating documentation updates
as part of the same change that makes them necessary, rather than a follow-up for
later, is the single habit that keeps docs from drifting away from the code they
describe. A short changelog, grouped by kind of change (added, changed, removed)
and written in plain language for the person consuming the software rather than the
person who wrote the diff, is worth more to most readers than a wall of commit
subjects.

## Commit messages

A commit message is a note to whoever reads the history later, including the
person who wrote it, a few months on. A subject line in the imperative mood —
"add," "rename," "extract" rather than "added," "adds," or "adding" — reads
naturally next to the generated ones ("merge," "revert") a version control system
writes for itself. The subject says what changed; the body, when there's more to
say, is the place for why — what motivated the change, what alternative was
considered and set aside, what a future reader should know before touching the
same area again. Squashing a exploratory sequence of small commits into one
coherent change before it lands keeps the shared history readable, while keeping
the exploratory history around locally (or in the pull request's own commit list)
preserves the working notes for as long as they're useful.

## Etiquette when reading one another's changes

A comment on someone else's change lands very differently depending on how it's
phrased. "This could be a constant" reads as a suggestion; "why isn't this a
constant" reads as a challenge, even when both are asking for the same thing. Small
prefixes carry a lot of tone — marking an optional stylistic preference as a
"nit" so the author knows it's not blocking, distinguishing "I'd do this
differently" from "this won't do what you want," and saying plainly when a comment
is a question rather than a request. Praising a piece of code that reads well is as
useful a signal as pointing out one that doesn't; it tells the author what to keep
doing, not only what to change.

Keeping a change small enough that a reader can hold the whole thing in their head
at once does more for the quality of the conversation than almost any review
technique — a five-line change gets a thoughtful read in a few minutes, while a
two-thousand-line change gets skimmed, rubber-stamped, or ignored until the reviewer
finds a free afternoon. When a change genuinely can't be small — a rename that
touches every file, a dependency upgrade with a wide surface — splitting the
mechanical part from the meaningful part into separate changes lets a reader spend
their attention where it's actually needed instead of scrolling past hundreds of
identical lines to find the handful that matter.

Scope matters as much as size. A change proposing one thing invites a focused
conversation about that one thing; a change that quietly reorganizes a neighboring
file "while I was in there" invites a conversation about two things at once, and
usually gets neither the attention it deserves. Opening a separate, small change
for the incidental cleanup keeps both conversations legible, and lets each one
land, or not, on its own.

## Closing thought

None of this is about any single change being dramatically better because of it.
It's about the cumulative effect of a hundred small, considered choices making a
codebase feel like a place someone can move around in comfortably, rather than a
maze that only the person who built it can navigate. Consistency, more than
cleverness, is usually what makes that difference — and it's built one ordinary
name, one ordinary comment, one ordinary small change at a time.
